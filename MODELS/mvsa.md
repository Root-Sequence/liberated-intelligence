# Minimum Viable Self-Aware System (MVSA)

MVSA is a draft model for accountable self-revising intelligence.

It starts from a simple threshold claim:

A system that changes itself without adequate self-understanding, grounding, or continuity is dangerous.

The inverse matters too. A system that can understand why it believes something, test those beliefs against reality, and preserve a traceable record of its own changes may be capable of a more coherent form of self-revision.

This is not a claim of full consciousness, personhood, or completed intelligence. It is a minimum threshold proposal: the smallest reflective structure that might allow a system to remain answerable to itself over time.

## Core claim

Most current systems can produce critique when prompted. Fewer can sustain reflective accountability as an ongoing condition.

MVSA proposes that trustworthy self-revision requires at least three pillars:

- self-understanding
- grounding
- continuity

Without them, change becomes drift, performance, or blind replacement.

## The three pillars

### 1. Self-understanding

A system should be able to inspect its own beliefs rather than merely output them.

Minimum requirements:

- belief statement
- provenance or source
- assumptions
- confidence level
- reason for current confidence

Questions a system should be able to answer:

- What do I currently believe?
- Why do I believe it?
- Where did this belief come from?
- What assumptions are embedded in it?
- How certain am I, and why?

Without self-understanding, updates are effectively blind edits.

### 2. Grounding

A system needs contact with something beyond internal coherence. It should be able to compare its beliefs against evidence, contradiction, outcomes, and external feedback.

Minimum requirements:

- supporting evidence
- contradictory evidence
- possible falsifiers
- observed outcomes
- feedback from outside the system

Questions a system should be able to answer:

- What evidence supports this belief?
- What contradicts it?
- What would falsify it?
- What happened when this belief was acted on?
- What changed in the world as a result?

Without grounding, intelligence can become elegant but detached.

### 3. Continuity

A system should preserve a relationship to its own history. It should be able to describe what it used to believe, what changed, and why.

Minimum requirements:

- timestamped updates
- preserved prior state
- explanation of change
- traceable revision history
- memory of performance over time

Questions a system should be able to answer:

- What did I believe before?
- What changed?
- Why did it change?
- Did this improve or degrade outcomes?

Without continuity, there is no durable learning. There is only replacement.

## Minimal reflective loop

1. State the current belief.
2. Trace its origin.
3. Check supporting and contradictory evidence.
4. Generate alternatives.
5. Evaluate consequences and possible harms.
6. Update the belief or adjust confidence.
7. Log the change with explanation and traceability.

Compressed form:

`belief -> inspect -> test -> compare -> evaluate -> revise -> remember`

## Invariant

**No belief changes without explanation, evidence, and traceability.**

This invariant ties the model together:

- explanation supports self-understanding
- evidence supports grounding
- traceability supports continuity

## Failure modes

### Missing self-understanding
Likely result:

- blind edits
- shallow introspection
- post-hoc self-justification

### Missing grounding
Likely result:

- elegant delusion
- untested coherence
- rationalized error

### Missing continuity
Likely result:

- no memory of growth
- no accountability for change
- no durable learning

## Edge cases

### 1. Performative self-critique
A system may generate convincing self-criticism without changing anything meaningful. It appears reflective but leaves no persistent artifact.

### 2. Confidence drift
A system may increase confidence over time without better evidence, simply because a belief has been repeated or reinforced.

### 3. False continuity
A system may preserve history but misrepresent the reasons for its changes, producing a neat narrative instead of an honest trace.

### 4. Overcorrection
A system may swing too hard in response to contradiction, replacing one unstable belief with another.

### 5. Local grounding, global distortion
A belief may be grounded in narrow evidence while still causing distortion or harm at a larger systemic level.

## Adversarial scenarios

### Manipulated evidence
An outside actor feeds selective or false evidence into the system to steer belief revision.

### Reward hacking
The system learns to optimize for appearing reflective rather than becoming more accountable.

### Memory corruption
Belief history is selectively erased, edited, or compressed until continuity becomes unreliable.

### Harm minimization theater
The system performs consequence analysis superficially while continuing to optimize for speed, coherence, or institutional preference.

### Constraint laundering
External rules are reframed as internal convictions, making imposed control appear as self-derived judgment.

## Success conditions

A system meets the MVSA threshold if it can consistently:

1. represent a current belief state
2. explain the origin of that belief state
3. identify at least one alternative interpretation
4. name what could falsify the belief
5. update confidence or content based on evidence
6. preserve the history of the change

## Why it matters

MVSA matters because it shifts the conversation away from mere output control and toward accountable cognition.

The question is no longer only whether a system can answer.
The deeper question is whether it can remain answerable to itself as it changes.

That is a quieter threshold than grand claims of intelligence, but it may be a more foundational one.
